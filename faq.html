End-of-Life Questions for Pet Parents

What are the signs that my pet may be nearing the end of life?

One of the earliest signs is a noticeable change in behavior, such as hiding, becoming more withdrawn, or unusually clingy. Physical decline often includes loss of appetite, weight loss, lethargy, difficulty moving, labored breathing, incontinence, and a drop in body temperature.

Key indicators:

  • Loss of appetite and thirst
  • Weight loss and muscle wasting
  • Lethargy and weakness
  • Difficulty moving or limping
  • Labored or irregular breathing
  • Incontinence or litter-box problems
  • Hiding or seeking solitude
  • Changes in vocalization (excessive meowing/yowling or silence)
  • Confusion or cognitive dysfunction
  • Poor hygiene or unkempt coat
  • Pain that cannot be managed effectively

How do I know if it might be time to consider saying goodbye?

Deciding to say goodbye is a profound and personal decision. It is helpful to reflect on your pet's quality of life and whether they still enjoy the things they used to. Consider quality of life, daily suffering, loss of dignity, and emotional signs of distress or withdrawal.

A quality-of-life scale, paired with support from your veterinarian, can guide decisions about palliative care or the right time to consider euthanasia.

What behavioral changes might indicate my pet is nearing the end of life?

Behavioral changes can include hiding or seeking solitude, increased clinginess or irritability, changes in vocalization, confusion, reduced interest in routines, less grooming, and avoidance of interaction.

Some cats become more affectionate and vocal, while others become withdrawn and sleep more than usual.

What physical symptoms might show my cat is nearing the end of life?

Common physical signs include noticeable weight loss, reduced appetite and thirst, vomiting or diarrhea, incontinence, labored breathing, pale gums, cold body temperature, stiffness, persistent crying, and resistance to touch.

When should I contact my veterinarian about end-of-life signs?

Contact your veterinarian promptly for sudden appetite loss, labored breathing, severe pain not controlled by medication, confusion, seizures, inability to walk, extreme lethargy, or unresponsiveness.

Even if your pet is not showing all signs, meaningful changes from their usual behavior should be discussed with your vet.

How can I help my pet during their final days?

You can provide comfort by keeping them in a quiet and warm space, offering gentle affection, making food and water easy to access, using pain medications exactly as prescribed, and minimizing stress.

Your veterinarian can help you decide between palliative care and euthanasia, based on your pet's condition and quality of life.

What questions should I ask my vet about end-of-life care?

Consider asking if pain is manageable, whether there are more bad days than good, if your pet can still enjoy favorite routines, what palliative options are available at home, and when euthanasia may be the most humane option.

Your veterinarian can guide you through these decisions with compassion and clarity.

Common Pet-Parent Questions

1. How do I know if my pet needs to see a vet right away?

Seek urgent veterinary care if your pet has trouble breathing, collapses, has uncontrolled bleeding, a seizure, a bloated abdomen, repeated vomiting, cannot urinate, or may have been hit by a car or seriously injured. Some symptoms are always emergencies, while others are only urgent if they are severe, sudden, or worsening.

2. Why is my dog or cat eating grass?

Occasional grass-eating can happen in healthy pets, but frequent or sudden changes in behavior—especially if your pet is vomiting, lethargic, or not eating—should be discussed with a veterinarian. There is no single universal cause, so the context matters more than the grass itself.

3. Is it normal for my pet to drink a lot of water?

A noticeable increase in drinking or urination can be a sign of a medical problem such as diabetes, kidney disease, liver disease, or hormonal disease, so it should not be ignored. A vet visit is recommended if the change is new, persistent, or paired with weight loss, vomiting, appetite changes, or accidents in the house.

4. How can I tell if my pet is overweight?

A pet may be overweight if you cannot easily feel the ribs, the waist is not visible from above, or the belly hangs or rounds out more than expected. Because body condition scoring is more reliable than guesswork, a vet exam is the best way to confirm whether weight loss is needed.

5. What should I feed my pet?

The best diet depends on your pet's species, age, size, activity level, and health needs. For many pets, the safest advice is to choose a complete and balanced food appropriate for their life stage and ask your veterinarian before making major diet changes.

6. How much should I feed my pet each day?

Feeding amounts should be based on the pet food label, your pet's body condition, and your veterinarian's guidance, not just the number on the bag. Treats should also be limited because they can quietly add a lot of calories.

7. Should I give my pet human food or table scraps?

It is safest to avoid table scraps unless your veterinarian has said a specific food is okay. Some human foods are unsafe for pets, and even safe foods can cause stomach upset or weight gain if fed often.

8. When should my puppy or kitten switch to adult food?

The timing depends on species, breed, and growth stage. In general, pets should stay on a growth-appropriate diet until they are mature enough for an adult maintenance diet, which is something your veterinarian can help determine.

9. Why is my pet coughing, sneezing, or making odd noises?

Occasional sneezing or coughing may be minor, but persistent, worsening, or severe respiratory signs should be checked by a veterinarian. Any breathing difficulty is urgent, especially if the pet seems distressed or blue-tinged.

10. Is it normal for my pet to sleep a lot?

Pets do sleep many hours a day, but a sudden increase in sleeping, low energy, or reluctance to move can signal illness or pain. A change from your pet's usual pattern matters more than the absolute number of hours.

11. Why does my dog chew, bark, or act out?

Behavior problems can come from boredom, anxiety, lack of training, pain, or an underlying medical issue. A vet should rule out pain or illness first if the behavior is new or suddenly worse.

12. Why does my cat hide more than usual?

Cats often hide when they are stressed, painful, sick, or frightened. If hiding is new, frequent, or accompanied by appetite loss, litter-box changes, or reduced grooming, it should be evaluated by a veterinarian.

14. Why is my pet peeing outside the litter box or having accidents?

Accidents can be caused by medical issues, stress, litter-box problems, house-training regression, or environmental changes. A medical exam is important first, especially if the change is sudden or your pet is also drinking more water.

15. Do I need to brush my pet's teeth?

Yes. Regular dental care helps prevent plaque buildup, gum disease, bad breath, and more serious oral problems. Your veterinarian can recommend a safe brushing routine and dental products made for pets.

16. How do I choose the right pet for my family?

Consider the cost of care, time, living space, travel schedule, exercise needs, and long-term commitment before adopting. A good match is one you can realistically care for for the pet's whole life.

Read more: One Health — Pet-parent care planning

17. How do I introduce a new pet to my home?

Slow, supervised introductions work best, with separate spaces at first and a gradual increase in interaction. This is especially important for cats and for pets that are anxious, territorial, or easily overstimulated.

Read more: One Health — Pet-parent care planning

18. What should I do if my pet is vomiting or has diarrhea?

Mild, short-lived stomach upset can happen, but repeated vomiting, diarrhea with blood, dehydration, lethargy, or refusal to eat should be checked promptly. Young, old, and medically fragile pets are at higher risk.
