When to See the Vet & Emergencies

How do I know if my pet needs to see a vet right away?

Seek urgent care if your pet has trouble breathing, collapses, has uncontrolled bleeding, a seizure, a bloated or hard abdomen, repeated vomiting, cannot urinate, or may have been hit by a car or seriously injured. Some signs are always emergencies; others are urgent only if they are severe, sudden, or getting worse.

  • Difficulty breathing or choking
  • Collapse, seizure, or unresponsiveness
  • Uncontrolled bleeding or major injury
  • Bloated, hard, or painful abdomen
  • Repeated vomiting or inability to urinate

Why is my pet coughing, sneezing, or breathing oddly?

Occasional sneezing or coughing may be minor, but persistent, worsening, or severe respiratory signs should be checked. Any breathing difficulty is urgent, especially if your pet seems distressed or their gums or tongue look blue-tinged.

What should I do if my pet is vomiting or has diarrhea?

Mild, short-lived stomach upset can happen, but repeated vomiting, diarrhea with blood, dehydration, lethargy, or refusal to eat should be checked promptly. Puppies, kittens, seniors, and pets with health conditions should be seen sooner.

Eating, Diet & Weight

What should I feed my pet?

The best diet depends on your pet's species, age, size, activity level, and health. For most pets, choose a complete and balanced food appropriate for their life stage, and check with your veterinarian before making major diet changes.

How much should I feed my pet each day?

Base feeding amounts on the food label, your pet's body condition, and your veterinarian's guidance, not just the number on the bag. Limit treats, since they can quietly add a lot of calories.

How can I tell if my pet is overweight?

Your pet may be overweight if you can't easily feel the ribs, the waist isn't visible from above, or the belly rounds out more than expected. A vet exam with body-condition scoring is the most reliable way to confirm whether weight loss is needed.

Should I give my pet human food or table scraps?

It's safest to avoid table scraps unless your veterinarian has approved a specific food. Some human foods are toxic to pets, and even safe foods can cause stomach upset or weight gain if fed often.

When should my puppy or kitten switch to adult food?

The timing depends on species, breed, and growth stage. In general, keep your pet on a growth-appropriate diet until they're mature enough for an adult maintenance diet — your veterinarian can help you decide.

Why is my dog or cat eating grass?

Occasional grass-eating can happen in healthy pets. But frequent grass-eating, or a sudden change paired with vomiting, lethargy, or not eating, is worth discussing with your veterinarian — the context matters more than the grass itself.

Drinking, Litter Box & House Habits

Is it normal for my pet to drink a lot of water?

A noticeable increase in drinking or urination can signal conditions like diabetes, kidney disease, or hormonal disease, so it shouldn't be ignored. See us if the change is new or persistent, or comes with weight loss, vomiting, appetite changes, or accidents in the house.

Why is my pet urinating outside the litter box or having accidents?

Accidents can be caused by medical issues, stress, litter-box problems, house-training regression, or changes at home. A medical exam comes first, especially if the change is sudden or your pet is also drinking more water.

Behavior & Sleep

Is it normal for my pet to sleep a lot?

Pets do sleep many hours a day, but a sudden increase in sleeping, low energy, or reluctance to move can signal illness or pain. A change from your pet's usual pattern matters more than the exact number of hours.

Why does my dog chew, bark, or act out?

Behavior problems can come from boredom, anxiety, lack of training, pain, or an underlying medical issue. If the behavior is new or suddenly worse, a vet should rule out pain or illness first.

Why does my cat hide more than usual?

Cats often hide when they're stressed, in pain, sick, or frightened. If hiding is new or frequent, or comes with appetite loss, litter-box changes, or less grooming, have your cat evaluated.

Dental Care

Do I need to brush my pet's teeth?

Yes. Regular dental care helps prevent plaque buildup, gum disease, bad breath, and more serious oral problems. We can recommend a safe brushing routine and dental products made for pets.

Choosing & Introducing a Pet

How do I choose the right pet for my family?

Consider the cost of care, your time, living space, travel schedule, exercise needs, and the long-term commitment before adopting. A good match is one you can realistically care for through the pet's whole life.

How do I introduce a new pet to my home?

Slow, supervised introductions work best — separate spaces at first, then a gradual increase in interaction. This matters especially for cats and for pets that are anxious, territorial, or easily overstimulated.
